STOCKHOLM, June 16 — Sweden has passed a new immigration law that allows authorities to revoke residence permits from non-citizens who fail to meet a newly established "good conduct" standard, marking one of the country's most significant immigration policy changes in recent years.

The legislation, approved by parliament on Monday, broadens the circumstances under which migrants can lose the right to remain in Sweden. Previously, residence permits were typically revoked only in cases involving serious criminal offences or threats to national security.

Under the new rules, authorities may also consider a range of non-criminal behaviours, including tax evasion, welfare fraud, unpaid debts, undeclared work, and associations with extremist groups or criminal networks.

The Swedish government argues that residency should be tied to respecting Swedish laws and contributing positively to society. Officials say the measure is intended to strengthen public confidence in the immigration system and improve integration outcomes.

However, the law has sparked criticism from human rights organizations and legal experts, who argue that the criteria for determining "good conduct" are too vague. Critics warn that migrants could face deportation for actions that do not constitute criminal offences and say the legislation may create uncertainty about legal rights and protections.

Individuals affected by the law will have the right to appeal decisions through Sweden's migration court system.

The measure forms part of a broader effort by Sweden's government to tighten immigration rules following years of debate over integration, crime, and migration policy. Supporters view the law as a necessary tool to address abuse of the system, while opponents fear it could undermine legal certainty and disproportionately affect vulnerable groups.

The legislation is expected to come into force later this year.
