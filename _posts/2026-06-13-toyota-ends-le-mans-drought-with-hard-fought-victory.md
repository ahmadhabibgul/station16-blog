LE MANS, France, June 14 — Toyota returned to the top step of the podium at the 24 Hours of Le Mans on Sunday, securing victory after an intense battle with BMW and Cadillac throughout the iconic endurance race.

The Japanese manufacturer claimed its first overall Le Mans win since 2022 as Kamui Kobayashi guided the No. 7 Toyota GR010 Hybrid across the finish line after 24 demanding hours at Circuit de la Sarthe.

Kobayashi shared the winning car with Britain's Mike Conway and Dutch driver Nyck de Vries. The triumph marked the second Le Mans victory for both Kobayashi and Conway, while de Vries celebrated his first overall success in the prestigious event.

Toyota's route to victory was far from straightforward. BMW entered the race from pole position and remained a major contender throughout, while Cadillac appeared poised to challenge for the win deep into the race after leading during the closing stages.

However, consistency and reliability ultimately proved decisive. The No. 7 Toyota maintained its challenge despite multiple setbacks and crossed the line 10.9 seconds ahead of the No. 20 BMW driven by Sheldon van der Linde, René Rast and Robin Frijns.

Toyota completed a double podium finish as its No. 8 entry, shared by Sébastien Buemi, Brendon Hartley and Ryo Hirakawa, secured third place overall.

Cadillac's No. 12 car finished fourth, while Ferrari, which had won the previous three editions of the race, rounded out the top five with its No. 51 entry.

One of the race's pivotal moments came when Cadillac's No. 38 car suffered a power steering issue after running near the front of the field. The problem forced additional pit stops and ultimately led to the car's retirement, ending the hopes of drivers Sébastien Bourdais, Earl Bamber and Jack Aitken.

BMW also endured disappointment with its pole-sitting No. 15 machine. Driven by Kevin Magnussen, Raffaele Marciello and Dries Vanthoor, the car was forced to retire before reaching the finish.

Elsewhere, Polish squad Inter Europol Competition dominated the LMP2 category by securing the top two positions, while Corvette emerged victorious in the LMGT3 class thanks to the efforts of Ben Keating, Nicky Catsburg and Jonny Edgar.

The 94th running of the legendary endurance race featured 62 cars and 186 drivers. The event was officially started on Saturday by cycling legend Mark Cavendish, who waved the field away for another chapter in Le Mans history.

Toyota's victory adds a sixth overall Le Mans title to the manufacturer's record and re-establishes the team as the benchmark in endurance racing's premier Hypercar category.
