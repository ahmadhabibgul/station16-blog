The United Kingdom has announced plans to ban children under the age of 16 from accessing major social media platforms, marking one of the most ambitious attempts yet by a Western government to address concerns over online safety, mental health, and digital addiction among young people.

Prime Minister Keir Starmer unveiled the policy during a press conference at Downing Street, describing it as a necessary step to protect children from the harmful effects of social media and to "give kids their childhood back." The restrictions are expected to apply to platforms including TikTok, Instagram, Snapchat, Facebook, X, YouTube, and Reddit, with implementation targeted for spring 2027.

The proposed legislation goes beyond Australia's under-16 social media ban by introducing additional safeguards. Under the plans, children under 16 would also be prohibited from livestreaming, while gaming platforms would face stricter controls to prevent adults from contacting minors. The government is also moving to restrict access to AI companion chatbots and other digital services deemed potentially harmful to young users.

Enforcement is expected to rely on age-verification technology overseen by Ofcom, the UK's communications regulator. Ministers are examining methods such as facial age estimation and digital identity checks, while also considering measures to prevent young users from circumventing restrictions through virtual private networks (VPNs).

Supporters of the policy argue that social media companies have failed to adequately protect children from harmful content, cyberbullying, and addictive algorithms. Government consultation data showed overwhelming parental support for stronger restrictions, with around nine in ten parents backing a minimum social media age of 16.

However, the announcement has sparked criticism from technology companies, digital rights groups, and disability advocates. Critics warn that a blanket ban could isolate young people who rely on online communities for support and friendship, particularly disabled children and those living in remote areas. Others argue that the focus should be on making platforms safer rather than banning access altogether.

Questions also remain over how effectively the ban can be enforced. Similar measures in Australia have faced challenges, with many underage users reportedly finding ways to bypass age restrictions. Ofcom has acknowledged that additional resources and clearer legislation may be required to ensure the UK's system operates as intended.

If approved by Parliament, the legislation would make the UK one of the first major nations to impose a near-complete social media ban on under-16s, setting the stage for a global debate about the role of governments, parents, and technology companies in protecting children online.
