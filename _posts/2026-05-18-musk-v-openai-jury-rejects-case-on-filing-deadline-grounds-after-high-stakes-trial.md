A federal jury in Oakland delivered a decisive defeat to Elon Musk on Monday, unanimously ruling that his lawsuit against OpenAI CEO Sam Altman and co-founder Greg Brockman was filed too late. District Judge Yvonne Gonzalez Rogers immediately adopted the verdict, bringing a three-week trial to a close without the jury addressing the substance of Musk’s allegations.

The case stemmed from Musk’s claim that Altman and Brockman abandoned OpenAI’s original charitable mission — established when Musk co-founded the organisation in 2015 — by transitioning it from a nonprofit structure toward a for-profit model. Musk argued he contributed roughly $38 million on the understanding that OpenAI would remain committed to developing artificial intelligence “for the benefit of humanity,” rather than private gain.

### Why Musk Lost

The jury did not evaluate whether any breach of fiduciary duty or unjust enrichment occurred. Instead, it concluded Musk’s claims were filed outside the legal time limits. Under applicable law, breach of charitable trust claims carry a three-year limitation period, while unjust enrichment claims are limited to two years.

OpenAI’s legal team argued that Musk was aware — or should have been aware — of the contested structural changes years before filing suit in February 2024. Internal discussions about a for-profit subsidiary began as early as 2017, and the structure was formalised in 2019. Jury deliberations lasted under two hours.

### Three Weeks of Testimony

Musk testified for three days, describing his relationship with OpenAI in stages: early supporter, increasing sceptic, and eventual belief that Altman had “looted the nonprofit.” Under cross-examination, he stated he only became certain of wrongdoing in 2023, following Microsoft’s $10 billion investment in OpenAI’s for-profit arm.

A key moment came from Greg Brockman’s 2017 journal entries, in which he wrote about being “warm to steal the nonprofit” and acknowledged Musk’s likely interpretation that the organisation had not been fully transparent with him.

Microsoft CEO Satya Nadella also testified. On the eve of trial, Musk sent Brockman an email warning: “By the end of this week, you and Sam will be the most hated men in America.” No settlement was reached.

### What Comes Next

Musk has already indicated he will appeal, with his legal team planning to invoke the “continuing violation doctrine,” which can extend filing deadlines in cases involving ongoing conduct. The judge declined to include that instruction to the jury — a decision Musk is expected to challenge.

The ruling comes as both sides advance major commercial milestones. OpenAI recently completed a $122 billion fundraising round at a valuation exceeding $850 billion and is moving toward a potential public listing. Musk is simultaneously preparing a SpaceX IPO following its February merger with xAI at a combined valuation of $1.25 trillion. The legal dispute, however, is far from concluded.
